![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Feminism_symbol.svg/28px-Feminism_symbol.svg.png)

**Gender-critical feminism**, also known as **trans-exclusionary radical feminism** or **TERFism**,[[1]](#cite_note-Thurlow-1)[[2]](#cite_note-Williams-2)[[3]](#cite_note-3)[[4]](#cite_note-Bassi-4) is an ideology or movement that opposes what it refers to as "[gender ideology](/wiki/Gender_ideology "Gender ideology")".[[5]](#cite_note-Pearce-5) Gender-critical feminists believe that [sex](/wiki/Sex "Sex") is biological, immutable, and binary, and consider the concepts of [gender identity](/wiki/Gender_identity "Gender identity") and [gender self-identification](/wiki/Gender_self-identification "Gender self-identification") to be inherently oppressive constructs tied to [gender roles](/wiki/Gender_roles "Gender roles").[[6]](#cite_note-6)[[7]](#cite_note-:0-7) They reject [transgender](/wiki/Transgender "Transgender") and [non-binary](/wiki/Non-binary "Non-binary") identities, and view [trans women](/wiki/Trans_women "Trans women") as men and [trans men](/wiki/Trans_men "Trans men") as women.[[8]](#cite_note-Burns_2019-8)

Originating as a [fringe](/wiki/Fringe_movement "Fringe movement") movement within [radical feminism](/wiki/Radical_feminism "Radical feminism") mainly in the United States,[[4]](#cite*note-Bassi-4)[[9]](#cite_note-Thomsen-9)[[10]](#cite_note-BD-10) trans-exclusionary radical feminism has achieved prominence in the United Kingdom[[11]](#cite_note-HinesSocRev-11) and South Korea,[[12]](#cite_note-:7-12)[[13]](#cite_note-:4-13) where it has been at the centre of high-profile controversies. It has been linked to promotion of [disinformation](/wiki/Disinformation "Disinformation")[[14]](#cite_note-:1-14)[[15]](#cite_note-:2-15)[[16]](#cite_note-:3-16) and to the [anti-gender movement](/wiki/Anti-gender_movement "Anti-gender movement").[[17]](#cite_note-Council_of_Europe_2021-17) Anti-gender rhetoric has seen increasing circulation in gender-critical feminist discourse since 2016, including use of the term "[gender ideology](/wiki/Gender_ideology "Gender ideology")".[[5]](#cite_note-Pearce-5) In several countries, gender-critical feminist groups have formed alliances with right-wing, [far-right](/wiki/Far-right "Far-right"), and [anti-feminist](/wiki/Anti-feminist "Anti-feminist") organisations.[[18]](#cite_note-Butler_idea_of*'gender'_backlash-18)[[19]](#cite_note-CTV_hate_rise_Canada-19)[[20]](#cite_note-Alm_&\_Engebretsen_2020-20)[[21]](#cite_note-Studnik-21)

Gender-critical feminism has been described as [transphobic](/wiki/Transphobic "Transphobic") by feminist and scholarly critics.[[1]](#cite_note-Thurlow-1)[[4]](#cite_note-Bassi-4) It is opposed by many feminist, [LGBT rights](/wiki/LGBT_rights_organization "LGBT rights organization"), and [human rights organizations](/wiki/Human_rights_organization "Human rights organization").[[22]](#cite_note-Canada_110-22)[[23]](#cite_note-Toutes_des_femmes-23) The [Council of Europe](/wiki/Council_of_Europe "Council of Europe") has condemned gender-critical ideology, among other ideologies, and linked it to "virulent attacks on the rights of [LGBTI](/wiki/LGBTI "LGBTI") people" in Hungary, Poland, Russia, Turkey, the United Kingdom, and other countries.[[24]](#cite_note-Resolution2417-24) [UN Women](/wiki/UN_Women "UN Women") has described the gender-critical movement, among other movements, as extreme [anti-rights movements](/wiki/Anti-rights_movements "Anti-rights movements") that employ [hate propaganda](/wiki/Hate_propaganda "Hate propaganda") and disinformation.[[25]](#cite_note-unwomen-25)[[26]](#cite_note-thetimesantirights-26)

## Terminology

### Trans-exclusionary radical feminism

Trans-inclusive [cisgender](/wiki/Cisgender "Cisgender") radical feminist [blogger](/wiki/Blogger "Blogger") Viv Smythe has been credited with popularizing the term "trans-exclusionary radical feminism" in 2008 as an online [shorthand](/wiki/Shorthand "Shorthand").[[27]](#cite_note-Smythe_2018-27) It was used to describe a minority of [feminists](/wiki/Feminists "Feminists")[[28]](#cite_note-Stryker_TSQ-28) who espouse sentiments that other feminists consider [transphobic](/wiki/Transphobic "Transphobic"),[[29]](#cite_note-Dastagir_2017-29)[[30]](#cite_note-30) including the rejection of the mainstream feminist view that trans women are women,[[8]](#cite_note-Burns_2019-8) opposition to [transgender rights](/wiki/Transgender_rights "Transgender rights"),[[8]](#cite_note-Burns_2019-8) and the exclusion of trans women in women's spaces and organizations.[[31]](#cite_note-O'Connell_2019-31) Smythe has also been credited with having coined the acronym "TERF", due to a blog post she wrote reacting to the [Michigan Womyn's Music Festival](/wiki/Michigan_Womyn%27s_Music_Festival "Michigan Womyn's Music Festival")'s [policy](/wiki/Michigan_Womyn%27s_Music_Festival#"Womyn-born_womyn"_policy "Michigan Womyn's Music Festival") of denying admittance to trans women. Though it was created as a deliberately neutral descriptor, "TERF" is now often considered [derogatory](/wiki/Derogatory "Derogatory") or dismissive, but may also be used as a self-description.[[32]](#cite_note-TerfOED-32)

Serena Bassi and Greta LaFleur write that "the argument by trans-exclusionary radical feminists that the term TERF (an acronym for 'trans-exclusionary radical feminist') is a 'slur'— rather than a description of a particular approach to politics—leans on a 'politics of injury' that distances itself from the real and very harmful work trans-exclusionary radical feminism is doing in the world."[[4]](#cite*note-Bassi-4) Cristan Williams writes in \_The SAGE Encyclopedia of Trans Studies*:

"The term has been rhetorically helpful in distinguishing TERF activism from the long-term radical feminist community members who are inclusive of trans women.  
 .... However, TERF activists characterize the acronym as offensive, which spawned the "TERF is a slur" campaign to discourage its use. Many TERFs see no distinction between their politics and radical feminist analysis, but if such a distinction is to be made, they encourage opinion leaders to use the term _gender critical_ instead of _TERF_.  
 .... While _TERF_, as a lexical unit, can carry unflattering overtones in the same way that _bigot_, _misogynist_, or _racist_ might, it nonetheless constructs a much-needed way to disentangle the sometimes subjugative, violent, and even murderous anti-trans behavior and rhetoric of TERF activism from radical feminism itself, even as TERFs work tirelessly to represent their activism as "radical feminism" on popular, social, and academic media platforms."[[33]](#cite_note-SAGE-TERF-33)

### Gender-critical feminism

Claire Thurlow said that since the 2010s, there has been a shift in language from "TERF" to "gender critical feminism", which she described as a [dog whistle](/wiki/Dog-whistle_politics "Dog-whistle politics") for anti-trans politics.[[1]](#cite_note-Thurlow-1) Researcher Aleardo Zanghellini argues that "gender-critical feminism advocates reserving women's spaces for [cis](/wiki/Cisgender "Cisgender") women".[[34]](#cite_note-Zanghellini_14-34) [Mauro Cabral Grinspan](/wiki/Mauro_Cabral_Grinspan "Mauro Cabral Grinspan"), Ilana Eloit, [David Paternotte](/wiki/David_Paternotte "David Paternotte") and [Mieke Verloo](/wiki/Mieke_Verloo "Mieke Verloo") describe "gender-critical feminism" as a "self-definition by some individuals and groups labelled TERFs" and argue that the term is problematic because it serves to rebrand anti-trans activism.[[35]](#cite_note-Grinspan-35)

## Views

### Sex and gender

Gender-critical feminists equate "women" with what they consider to be a "female sex class", and view historical and contemporary oppression of women as being rooted in their being female, while "gender" is a system of social norms which functions to oppress women on the basis of their sex.[[7]](#cite*note-:0-7)[[36]](#cite_note-36)[[37]](#cite_note-37) They believe sex is biological and cannot be changed,[[38]](#cite_note-38) and that equity legislation protecting against discrimination based on sex should be interpreted as solely referring to biological sex.[[39]](#cite_note-39)[*[better source needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS "Wikipedia:NOTRS")\_] Furthermore, gender critics emphasise the view that sex is binary,[[40]](#cite_note-40) as opposed to a continuous spectrum, and that the two sexes have an objective, material basis as opposed to being socially constructed.[[41]](#cite_note-stock_material_girls_ch2-41)

Gender-critical feminists promote the idea that sex is important.[[42]](#cite*note-42)[[43]](#cite_note-43)[[44]](#cite_note-44) In *[Material Girls](/wiki/Material_Girls:_Why_Reality_Matters_for_Feminism "Material Girls: Why Reality Matters for Feminism")_, [Kathleen Stock](/wiki/Kathleen_Stock "Kathleen Stock") discusses four areas in which she expresses the view that sex-associated differences are important, regardless of gender: [medicine](/wiki/Sex_differences_in_medicine "Sex differences in medicine"), sport, [sexual orientation](/wiki/Sexual_orientation "Sexual orientation"), and the social effects of heterosexuality (such as [gender pay gaps](/wiki/Gender_pay_gap "Gender pay gap") and [sexual assault](/wiki/Sexual_assault "Sexual assault")).[[45]](#cite_note-45) [Holly Lawford-Smith](/wiki/Holly_Lawford-Smith "Holly Lawford-Smith") states: "Gender critical feminism is \_not_ 'about' trans. It is _about_ sex."[[46]](#cite*note-hls_what_is_gcf-46) Lawford-Smith said of gender-critical feminism: "It is about being critical of gender, and this has implications for a wide range of feminist issues, not just gender identity." Writing of her view of a "gender-critical feminist utopia", she said: "While there will still be the same \_people* who think of themselves as 'transmen', 'transwomen' or 'non-binary' today, they will not use those labels, because 'feminine' will be a way that males can be, 'masculine' will be a way that women can be, and 'androgynous' will be a way that anyone can be."[[47]](#cite_note-H_L_Smith_2022-47)

In gender-critical discourse, the terms _man_ and _woman_ are used as sex-terms, assigned no more meaning than _adult human male_ and _adult human female_ respectively, in contrast to feminist theorists who argue these terms embody a social category distinct from matters of biology (usually referred to as _gender_), with _masculinity_ and _femininity_ representing normative characteristics thereof.[[48]](#cite*note-48)[[49]](#cite_note-49) The phrase *[adult human female](/wiki/Adult_human_female "Adult human female")\_ has become a [slogan](/wiki/Slogan "Slogan") in gender-critical politics, and has been described as transphobic.[[50]](#cite_note-50)

### "Sex-based rights"

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/TERF_sticker.jpg/250px-TERF_sticker.jpg)

Gender critical feminists advocate what they call "sex-based rights", arguing that "women's human rights are based upon sex" and that "these rights are being eroded by the promotion of 'gender identity'".[[11]](#cite_note-HinesSocRev-11)

Human rights scholar Sandra Duffy described the concept of "sex-based rights" as "a fiction with the pretense of legality", noting that the word "sex" in [international human rights law](/wiki/International_human_rights_law "International human rights law") does not share the implications of the word "sex" in gender-critical discourse and is widely agreed to also refer to [gender](/wiki/Gender "Gender").[[51]](#cite_note-51) [Catharine A. MacKinnon](/wiki/Catharine_A._MacKinnon "Catharine A. MacKinnon") noted that "the recognition [that discrimination against trans people is discrimination on the basis of sex, that is gender, the social meaning of sex] does not, contrary to allegations of anti-trans self-identified feminists, endanger women or feminism", they expand by saying "women do not have 'sex-based rights' in the affirmative sense some in this group seem to think".[[52]](#cite_note-52) The term has been adopted by [Donald Trump](/wiki/Donald_Trump "Donald Trump") and was used in an executive order titled "[Defending Women from Gender Ideology Extremism and Restoring Biological Truth to the Federal Government](/wiki/Defending_Women_from_Gender_Ideology_Extremism_and_Restoring_Biological_Truth_to_the_Federal_Government "Defending Women from Gender Ideology Extremism and Restoring Biological Truth to the Federal Government")", which seeks to erase official recognition of transgender people and roll back their protections.[[53]](#cite_note-inde-53)[[54]](#cite_note-54)

### Inclusive language

Scholars Lucy Jones and Rodrigo Borba have published work stating that gender-critical actors often resist the adoption of [inclusive and nonbinary language](/wiki/Inclusive_language "Inclusive language"), particularly in relation to [pronouns](/wiki/Preferred_gender_pronoun "Preferred gender pronoun") and the recognition of transgender and nonbinary identities. In her 2023 review of literature on language, gender, and sexuality, Lucy Jones says that gender-critical feminists frequently reject linguistic practices that affirm trans and nonbinary identities, often citing the preservation of "sex-based rights" as justification. She says that this resistance is typically framed by a binary and essentialist ideology that defines "woman" exclusively as someone assigned female at birth.[[55]](#cite_note-Jones-55) Drawing on this scholarship, Jones characterizes gender-critical resistance to inclusive language as part of a broader "cisnormative preoccupation with trans people's bodies" and a form of linguistic policing aimed at denying the legitimacy of trans and nonbinary identities.[[55]](#cite_note-Jones-55) Jones situates these discursive patterns within a wider political context by citing Borba (2022), who states that there has been an emergence of an "anti-gender register" used in trans-exclusionary discourse, including gender-critical feminism. Borba argues that this register, which draws on essentialist ideas about sex and gender, has gained traction through a process of enregisterment, a way of making certain ideological positions appear natural or commonsensical. He further suggests that this has been achieved in part by appropriating the language of feminist and LGBTQ+ antidiscrimination activism, reframing it to emphasize threats to the rights of cisgender women and children.[[55]](#cite_note-Jones-55)[[56]](#cite_note-Borba-56)

### Socialisation and gender nonconformity

Gender critical feminists generally see gender as a system in which women are oppressed for reasons intrinsically related to their sex, and emphasize male violence against women, particularly involving institutions such as the [sex industry](/wiki/Sex_industry "Sex industry"), as central to women's oppression.[[57]](#cite_note-McKay_2015-57)[[58]](#cite_note-58) Holders of such views often contend that trans women cannot fully be women because they were assigned male at birth and have experienced some degree of [male privilege](/wiki/Male_privilege "Male privilege").[[59]](#cite_note-59) [Germaine Greer](/wiki/Germaine_Greer "Germaine Greer") has said that it "wasn't fair" that "a man who has lived for 40 years as a man and had children with a woman and enjoyed the services—the unpaid services of a wife, which most women will never know…then decides that the whole time he's been a woman".[[60]](#cite_note-timegreer-60)

These ideas have been met with criticism from believers in other branches of feminism. Sociologist Patricia Elliot argues that the view that one's socialization as a girl or woman defines "women's experience" assumes that cis women's experiences are homogeneous and discounts the possibility that trans and cis women may share the experience of being disparaged for their perceived femininity.[[61]](#cite_note-Elliot_2004-61) Others argue that expectations of one's assigned sex are something enforced upon them, beginning at early socialization, and transgender youth, especially gender-nonconforming children, often experience different, worse treatment involving reprisals for their deviation therefrom.[[62]](#cite_note-Dietert_Dentice-62)

Transfeminist [Julia Serano](/wiki/Julia_Serano "Julia Serano") has referred to implying that trans women may experience some degree of male privilege pre-transition as "denying [them] [the closet](/wiki/The_closet "The closet")", and has compared it to saying that a cisgender gay person experienced straight privilege before coming out. She has also compared it to if a cisgender girl was raised as a boy against her will, and how the two scenarios tend to be viewed differently by a cisgender audience, despite being ostensibly similar experiences from a transfeminine perspective.[[63]](#cite_note-63)

### Gender transition

In _[The Transsexual Empire](/wiki/The_Transsexual_Empire "The Transsexual Empire")_ (1979), feminist Janice Raymond denounces the act of transition as "rape", by virtue of "reducing the real female form to an artifact, appropriating this body for themselves".[[64]](#cite_note-64) She stated "the problem of transsexualism would best be served by morally mandating it out of existence" and when later feminist criticism compared her writing to genocidal rhetoric, argued in 2014 that she didn't call for physical eradication but eradication of "the medical and social systems that support transsexualism and the reasons why in a gender-defined society, persons find it necessary to change their bodies".[[65]](#cite_note-Kosse-65)

In her own book _[Gyn/Ecology](/wiki/Gyn/Ecology "Gyn/Ecology")_ (1979), originally published one year earlier, [Mary Daly](/wiki/Mary_Daly "Mary Daly"), who had served as Raymond's thesis supervisor,[[66]](#cite_note-66) insisted that as sex reassignment surgery could not reproduce female chromosomes, the clitoris, the ability to give birth, the ability to menstruate, or a female life history, it could "not produce women".[[67]](#cite_note-Daly-67): 67–68 [Sheila Jeffreys](/wiki/Sheila_Jeffreys "Sheila Jeffreys") and [Germaine Greer](/wiki/Germaine_Greer "Germaine Greer") have made similar remarks.[[68]](#cite_note-BINDEL2-68) Daly presented gender transition as the result of a grotesque patriarchal urge to violate natural boundaries and imitate motherhood, assimilating it to a broader concept of "male motherhood" that also included the Catholic priesthood, and claimed that it represented a male technological attempt to replace women altogether.[[67]](#cite_note-Daly-67): 71–72 She also compared the idea that a trans woman could be a woman despite lacking a clitoris to the ideology behind "African [female genital mutilation](/wiki/Female_genital_mutilation "Female genital mutilation")".[[67]](#cite_note-Daly-67): 167

In a response to remarks by [Elizabeth Grosz](/wiki/Elizabeth_Grosz "Elizabeth Grosz"), philosopher [Eva Hayward](/wiki/Eva_Simone_Hayward "Eva Simone Hayward") characterized this type of view as telling trans people who have had sex reassignment surgery: "Don't exist."[[69]](#cite_note-69)

[Helen Joyce](/wiki/Helen_Joyce "Helen Joyce") has called for "reducing or keeping down the number of people who transition" because every one who does, happy or not, is a person who's "damaged" and "a huge problem to a sane world".[[65]](#cite_note-Kosse-65)[[70]](#cite_note-70)

### Intersex conditions

Radical feminist [Germaine Greer](/wiki/Germaine_Greer "Germaine Greer") called women with XY [AIS](/wiki/Androgen_insensitivity_syndrome "Androgen insensitivity syndrome") "men" and "incomplete males" in her 1999 book _The Whole Woman_. [Iain Morland](/wiki/Iain_Morland "Iain Morland") responded that "in trying to criticize the social construction of femaleness and intersex, Greer disenfranchised precisely those people who live at the intersection of the two categories".[[71]](#cite_note-71)[[72]](#cite_note-72) Greer admitted in 2016 that defining men and women solely using chromosomes was wrong.[[60]](#cite_note-timegreer-60) Later gender-critical feminists have disputed the prevalence of intersex conditions, arguing that [Anne Fausto-Sterling](/wiki/Anne_Fausto-Sterling "Anne Fausto-Sterling")'s estimate of 1.7% comprises mostly cases not normally considered ambiguous "in genitalia or in reproductive organs", like [nonclassic CAH](/wiki/Nonclassic_congenital_adrenal_hyperplasia "Nonclassic congenital adrenal hyperplasia"), [Turner syndrome](/wiki/Turner_syndrome "Turner syndrome"), or [Klinefelter syndrome](/wiki/Klinefelter_syndrome "Klinefelter syndrome").[[41]](#cite_note-stock_material_girls_ch2-41) Citing research showing much lower prevalence, Kathleen Stock and Holly Lawford-Smith have both argued that the existence of intersex conditions does not impact the usefulness of sex categories,[[46]](#cite_note-hls_what_is_gcf-46)[[73]](#cite_note-econ_stock-73) with Lawford-Smith saying that the term "assigned female at birth" has been "appropriated from people with differences of sexual development", and "used by trans activists for everyone, even though in more than 99% of cases, as we have seen, sex is accurately observed, not 'assigned'".[[46]](#cite_note-hls_what_is_gcf-46)

Most intersex organizations subscribe to a mixed [sociological perspective of sex and gender](/wiki/Social_construction_of_gender "Social construction of gender"), and as trans legislation and subjects overlaps heavily with intersex legislation, intersex people are often involved in [trans activism](/wiki/Trans_activism "Trans activism").[[74]](#cite_note-74)[[75]](#cite_note-75) Intersex women who display a mixed sexual [phenotype](/wiki/Phenotype "Phenotype") often face [attacks similar to trans people](/wiki/Violence_against_transgender_people "Violence against transgender people").[[76]](#cite_note-76)[[77]](#cite_note-77)

### Sexual orientation

Gender critical feminists believe that transgender rights are a threat to the rights of gay people.[[78]](#cite_note-Lamble-78) Gender critical lesbians and feminists are a minority in the UK: polls show that cisgender lesbians and bisexual women are among the most trans-inclusive groups in Britain.[[78]](#cite_note-Lamble-78)

[Kathleen Stock](/wiki/Kathleen_Stock "Kathleen Stock"), for instance, has said that allowing trans women to call themselves women "threatens a secure understanding of the concept 'lesbian'".[[73]](#cite_note-econ_stock-73) [Magdalen Berns](/wiki/Magdalen_Berns "Magdalen Berns"), co-founder of the group [For Women Scotland](/wiki/For_Women_Scotland "For Women Scotland"), has said that "there is no such thing as a lesbian with a penis" in regards to the idea of some trans women being lesbians.[[79]](#cite_note-79)

[Julie Bindel](/wiki/Julie_Bindel "Julie Bindel") has said that transgender women cannot be lesbians, instead qualifying them as straight men trying to "join the club", and has compared transgender activism to men sexually assaulting lesbian women for rejecting their advances.[[80]](#cite_note-80)[[81]](#cite_note-81)

Many other gender critical groups and pundits have spoken of the transgender rights movement as a men's sexual rights movement, designed to pressure lesbians into having sex with trans women.[[82]](#cite_note-82)[[83]](#cite_note-83)[[84]](#cite_note-84)

[Ray Blanchard's theory of autogynephilia](/wiki/Blanchard%27s_transsexualism_typology "Blanchard's transsexualism typology") is a recurrent talking point in TERF discourse, where it is usually presented as established science. It characterises trans women's gender identities as caused by sexual orientation or [sexual deviance](/wiki/Sexual_deviance "Sexual deviance").[[85]](#cite_note-:9-85) The theory has never received wide acceptance in sexology or psychology.[[85]](#cite_note-:9-85)

### Conversion therapy

Kathleen Stock has argued that definitions of conversion therapy and bans against it should not include [gender identity conversion therapy](</wiki/Conversion_therapy#Gender_identity_change_efforts_(GICE)> "Conversion therapy") on the basis that it risks criminalising "proper therapeutic exploration",[[86]](#cite_note-86) and that she believes it comes into conflict with bans against [sexual orientation conversion therapy](/wiki/Sexual_orientation_conversion_therapy "Sexual orientation conversion therapy").[[87]](#cite_note-Zanghellini-87) This latter argument has been criticized on the basis that doctors affirming transgender youth do not attempt to alter [sexual orientation](/wiki/Sexual_orientation "Sexual orientation"), which is understood to define who they are attracted to, and respect the person's expressed gender identity and sexual orientation.[[87]](#cite_note-Zanghellini-87) Gender-critical campaign groups in the United Kingdom such as Sex Matters have described the provision of gender-affirming care for transgender youth as "modern conversion therapy" which erases gay identities and argued it should be criminalized.[[88]](#cite_note-88)[[89]](#cite_note-89)[[90]](#cite_note-90) Trans-exclusionary radical feminists in France campaigned against a ban on conversion therapy arguing that most transgender teenagers assigned female at birth aren't really trans.[[91]](#cite_note-Fassin-91)

In March 2022, gender-critical groups campaigned to have the UK government remove gender identity change efforts from a [proposed ban on conversion therapy](/wiki/LGBT_rights_in_the_United_Kingdom#Conversion_therapy "LGBT rights in the United Kingdom").[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_][[92]](#cite_note-92)

The [Trevor Project](/wiki/Trevor_Project "Trevor Project") and [International Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual, Trans and Intersex Association](/wiki/International_Lesbian,_Gay,_Bisexual,_Trans_and_Intersex_Association "International Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual, Trans and Intersex Association") have stated "gender critical therapy" is another name for conversion therapy.[[93]](#cite_note-93)[[94]](#cite_note-ILGA-94) Heron Greenesmith has reported on gender critical boards sharing lists of therapists whose end goal is the rejection of trans identity for parents of trans youth.[[95]](#cite_note-Greenesmith-95) The gender-critical group [Genspect](/wiki/Genspect "Genspect") promotes "gender exploratory therapy", which is also considered to be [a form of conversion therapy](/wiki/Conversion_therapy#Gender_exploratory_therapy "Conversion therapy").[[96]](#cite_note-96) They argue that transgender identities stem from unprocessed trauma, childhood abuse, internalized homophobia or misogyny, sexual fetishism, and autism.[[97]](#cite_note-Santoro-97)

## History

### Early history (before 2000)

Although trans people were active in feminist movements in the 1960s and earlier,[[98]](#cite_note-98) the 1970s saw conflict among some early [radical feminists](/wiki/Radical_feminists "Radical feminists") over the inclusion of trans women in feminism.[[99]](#cite_note-Bettcher-99)[[100]](#cite_note-100)

In 1973, trans-exclusionary radical feminist activists from the [Daughters of Bilitis](/wiki/Daughters_of_Bilitis "Daughters of Bilitis") voted to expel [Beth Elliott](/wiki/Beth_Elliott "Beth Elliott"), an out trans woman, from the organization.[[33]](#cite_note-SAGE-TERF-33) The same year, Elliott was scheduled to perform at the West Coast Lesbian Conference, which she had helped organize; a group of trans-exclusionary radical feminist activists calling themselves the Gutter Dykes leafletted the conference protesting her inclusion and keynote speaker [Robin Morgan](/wiki/Robin_Morgan "Robin Morgan") updated her speech to describe Elliott as "an opportunist, an infiltrator, and a destroyer – with the mentality of a rapist".[[33]](#cite_note-SAGE-TERF-33)[[1]](#cite_note-Thurlow-1)[[101]](#cite_note-Williams_2020_pp.\_718–734-101) An impromptu vote was held with the majority supporting her inclusion in the conference; when Elliott subsequently entered the stage to perform the Gutter Dykes rushed to the stage to attack her and attacked performers [Robin Tyler](/wiki/Robin_Tyler "Robin Tyler") and [Patty Harrison](/wiki/Patty_Harrison "Patty Harrison") who had stepped in to defend her.[[33]](#cite_note-SAGE-TERF-33)[[1]](#cite_note-Thurlow-1)[[101]](#cite_note-Williams_2020_pp.\_718–734-101)

At the 1973 [Christopher Street Liberation Day](/wiki/Christopher_Street_Liberation_Day "Christopher Street Liberation Day") rally, trans-exclusionary radical feminists tried to stop [Sylvia Rivera](/wiki/Sylvia_Rivera "Sylvia Rivera") from speaking.[[33]](#cite_note-SAGE-TERF-33) [Jean O'Leary](/wiki/Jean_O%27Leary "Jean O'Leary") publicly denounced Sylvia Rivera as "parodying womanhood" and [Lesbian Feminist Liberation](/wiki/Lesbian_Feminist_Liberation "Lesbian Feminist Liberation") distributed flyers seeking to keep "female impersonators" off the stage.[[102]](#cite_note-102)

Trans-exclusionary radical feminist activists protested [Sandy Stone](</wiki/Sandy_Stone_(artist)> "Sandy Stone (artist)")'s position at [Olivia Records](/wiki/Olivia_Records "Olivia Records"), a trans-inclusive lesbian separatist music collective. In 1977 The Gorgons, a trans-exclusionary lesbian separatist paramilitary group, issued a death threat to Stone and came to the event armed though were intercepted by security. Escalating threats against the collective motivated Stone to leave the group.[[33]](#cite_note-SAGE-TERF-33)

[Janice Raymond](/wiki/Janice_Raymond "Janice Raymond")'s _[The Transsexual Empire](/wiki/The_Transsexual_Empire "The Transsexual Empire")_, published in 1979, examined what she considered to be the role of transgender identity in reinforcing traditional gender stereotypes, in particular the ways in which the "medical-psychiatric complex" was medicalizing gender identity, and the social and political context that contributed to the image of gender-affirming treatment and surgery as therapeutic medicine.[[103]](#cite_note-Raymond_1994-103) Raymond maintained that this was based in the "patriarchal myths" of "male mothering", and "making of woman according to man's image", and that transgender identity aimed "to colonize feminist identification, culture, politics and sexuality".[[103]](#cite_note-Raymond_1994-103) The book goes on to say that "All transsexuals [rape](/wiki/Rape "Rape") women's bodies by reducing the real female form to an artifact" and that "the problem of transsexualism would best be served by morally mandating it out of existence".[[104]](#cite_note-104) Several authors have since characterized this work as transphobic and constituting hate speech, as well as lacking any serious intellectual basis.[[105]](#cite_note-Rose_2004-105)[[106]](#cite_note-Namaste_2000-106)[[107]](#cite_note-Hayes_2003-107)[[108]](#cite_note-Serano_2007-108): 233–234

In 1991 Nancy Burkholder, a trans woman, was ejected from the [Michigan Womyn's Music Festival](/wiki/Michigan_Womyn%27s_Music_Festival "Michigan Womyn's Music Festival") (MWMF), after refusing to answer when another woman asked her whether or not she was transgender.[[109]](#cite_note-109)[[33]](#cite_note-SAGE-TERF-33) This removal was justified by the retroactive instatement of a [womyn-born womyn](/wiki/Womyn-born_womyn "Womyn-born womyn") policy by the MWMF organisers.[[108]](#cite_note-Serano_2007-108): 233–245 For both the 1992 and 1993 MWMF events, [Janis Walworth](/w/index.php?title=Janis_Walworth&action=edit&redlink=1 "Janis Walworth (page does not exist)"), a cisgender lesbian feminist, organised an educational and outreach program at the MWMF distributing pamphlets titled "Gender Myths".[[101]](#cite_note-Williams_2020_pp.\_718–734-101) During the 1993 MWMF event, Walworth was told by event security that she and any trans women in their group would be required to leave the event "for their own safety".[[101]](#cite_note-Williams_2020_pp.\_718–734-101) Although an offer of bodyguard protection was provided by a group of [leather lesbians](/wiki/Leather_subculture "Leather subculture") attending the festival, Walworth's group decided instead to set up an outreach camp outside the festival gates.[[101]](#cite_note-Williams_2020_pp.\_718–734-101)[[33]](#cite_note-SAGE-TERF-33) This camp, later known as [Camp Trans](/wiki/Camp_Trans "Camp Trans"), continued to provide education and outreach attempts while protesting the festival's trans exclusionary practices until the festival's final event in 2015.[[101]](#cite_note-Williams_2020_pp.\_718–734-101)[[33]](#cite_note-SAGE-TERF-33)

### Canada

Nurse Amy Hamm was found to have committed unprofessional conduct by the British Columbia College of Nurses and Midwives, following lengthy proceedings.[[110]](#cite_note-:10-110) She is appealing to the [British Columbia Supreme Court](/wiki/British_Columbia_Supreme_Court "British Columbia Supreme Court").[[110]](#cite_note-:10-110) Online comments made by the nurse between July 2018 and March 2021 were considered "discriminatory and derogatory statements" about transgender people. Hamm said she was not transphobic, but worried for the rights of women and girls.[[110]](#cite_note-:10-110)

### Russia

In Russia, trans-exclusionary feminists, who position themselves as [radical](/wiki/Radical_feminism "Radical feminism"), constitute one of the two main streams of [feminism](/wiki/Feminism_in_Russia "Feminism in Russia"). Unlike their opponents adhering to [intersectional feminism](/wiki/Intersectional_feminism "Intersectional feminism"), the trans-exclusionary group Womenation and a number of other trans-exclusionary feminists supported the [Russian invasion of Ukraine](/wiki/Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine "Russian invasion of Ukraine") in 2022 and came into conflict with [Ukrainian feminist](/wiki/Feminism_in_Ukraine "Feminism in Ukraine") movement. Vanya Mark Solovey, a gender researcher, argues that the solidarity of Russian trans-exclusionary feminists with Russian policy towards Ukraine is closely related to the anti-trans sentiments of the Russian authorities.[[111]](#cite_note-111)

### South Korea

In 2016, the [radical feminist](/wiki/Radical_feminist "Radical feminist") online community [Womad](</wiki/Womad_(website)> "Womad (website)") split from the larger radical feminist online community [Megalia](/wiki/Megalia "Megalia") after Megalia issued a ban on the use of certain explicit slurs against gay men and transgender people. This change in policy led to the migration of [anti-LGBT](/wiki/Anti-LGBT "Anti-LGBT") members.[[112]](#cite_note-:0b-112)

In February 2020, [Sookmyung Women's University](/wiki/Sookmyung_Women%27s_University "Sookmyung Women's University") accepted its first transgender student. The decision prompted a strong backlash both within and outside of the university, including from radical feminist student organizations. However, some students, and the university's Student and Minority Human Rights Commission, supported the decision.[[12]](#cite_note-:7-12)[[113]](#cite_note-113)[[114]](#cite_note-114)

Lee Hyun-Jae has noted that in the South Korean "[feminism reboot](/wiki/Feminism_reboot "Feminism reboot")" of the early 21st century, the radical stance of recent feminists have been "oriented in an [identity politics](/wiki/Identity_politics "Identity politics") based on [biological sex](/wiki/Biological_sex "Biological sex")", and that "the radical stance of today's [young] feminists has a tendency to emphasize the identity of the 'female body' as based on the category of the 'biological woman,' taking an attitude of excluding 'biological' men refugees, and transgender people".[[13]](#cite_note-:4-13) Jinsook Kim has noted that "in Korean contexts, there have been increasing concerns over popular forms of feminism based on a strong female identity rooted in notions of biological sex, the pursuit of female-only and -first politics, and the refusal of solidarity with other social minority groups".[[115]](#cite_note-:6-115)

### Sweden

In Sweden, scholars Karlberg, Korolczuk, and Sältenberg have stated that gender-critical discourse is part of the broader [anti-gender](/wiki/Anti-gender "Anti-gender") movement, which they argue contributes to the erosion of liberal democracy through exclusion and marginalization.[[116]](#cite_note-Karlberg-116) They describe such discourse as playing a role in what they call "insidious [de-democratization](/wiki/De-democratization "De-democratization")," a gradual weakening of liberal norms through the targeting of already vulnerable groups, including trans people.[[116]](#cite_note-Karlberg-116) One of the more prominent actors associated with this discourse is the [Swedish Women's Lobby](/wiki/Swedish_Women%27s_Lobby "Swedish Women's Lobby") (SWL), which has in recent years been criticized for adopting trans-exclusionary positions.[[116]](#cite_note-Karlberg-116)[[117]](#cite_note-Alm-117)[[118]](#cite_note-Grahn-118)[[119]](#cite_note-Ottar-119)
In 2021, 943 priests and employees of the [Church of Sweden](/wiki/Church_of_Sweden "Church of Sweden") condemned "trans-exclusionary feminism [that] uses rhetoric we recognize from radical right-wing Christian groups and right-wing populists," adding: "We mourn a rights movement that punches down. You, me, we, all of us, need a broad, solidarity-based feminism that fights restrictive gender norms."[[120]](#cite_note-svenskakyrkan-120)

In 2016, the [House of Commons](/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom "House of Commons of the United Kingdom")' [Women and Equalities Committee](/wiki/Women_and_Equalities_Committee "Women and Equalities Committee") issued a report recommending that the [Gender Recognition Act 2004](/wiki/Gender_Recognition_Act_2004 "Gender Recognition Act 2004") be updated "in line with the principles of [gender self-declaration](/wiki/Gender_self-declaration "Gender self-declaration")".[[121]](#cite_note-121) Later in 2016, in [England and Wales](/wiki/England_and_Wales "England and Wales"), a proposal was developed under [Theresa May](/wiki/Theresa_May "Theresa May")'s government to revise the Act to introduce self-identification, with a public consultation opening in 2018. This proposed reform became a key locus of conflict for the emerging gender-critical movement, seeking to block reform of the Act, with a number of groups such as [Fair Play For Women](/wiki/Fair_Play_For_Women "Fair Play For Women"), [For Women Scotland](/wiki/For_Women_Scotland "For Women Scotland"), and [Woman's Place UK](/wiki/Woman%27s_Place_UK "Woman's Place UK") being formed. 2018 found a significant majority of respondents in favour of the GRA reforms,[[122]](#cite_note-122) however, in 2020, [Boris Johnson's government](/wiki/Second_Johnson_ministry "Second Johnson ministry") dropped the reforms, instead reducing the cost of a [gender recognition certificate](/wiki/Gender_recognition_certificate "Gender recognition certificate") and moving the application process online.

Another key locus of conflict for the emerging movement was the stance of LGBT rights charity [Stonewall](</wiki/Stonewall_(charity)> "Stonewall (charity)") on trans issues. In 2015, Stonewall had begun campaigning for trans equality, with Stonewall head [Ruth Hunt](/wiki/Ruth_Hunt "Ruth Hunt") apologising for the organisation's previous failure to do so.[[123]](#cite_note-123) In 2019, the [LGB Alliance](/wiki/LGB_Alliance "LGB Alliance") was founded in opposition to Stonewall, accusing the organization of having "undermined women's sex-based rights and protections" and attempting "to introduce confusion between biological sex and the notion of gender".[[124]](#cite_note-124)

The year 2019 saw the formation of the [Women's Human Rights Campaign](/wiki/Women%27s_Human_Rights_Campaign "Women's Human Rights Campaign") (now Women's Declaration International) by noted gender-critical feminist [Sheila Jeffreys](/wiki/Sheila_Jeffreys "Sheila Jeffreys") and co-founder Heather Brunskell-Evans. The group published a manifesto titled the _Declaration on Women's Sex-Based Rights_, which argued that recognising trans women as women "constitutes discrimination against women" and called for the "elimination of that act".[[125]](#cite_note-125)[[126]](#cite_note-126)

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/J._K._Rowling_2010.jpg/250px-J._K._Rowling_2010.jpg)

A 2020 paper in [SAGE Open](/wiki/SAGE_Open "SAGE Open") said that "the case against trans inclusion in the United Kingdom has been presented primarily through social media and blog-type or journalistic online platforms lacking the traditional prepublication checks of academic peer review".[[127]](#cite_note-127) Some public figures such as [Graham Linehan](/wiki/Graham_Linehan "Graham Linehan")[[128]](#cite_note-128)[[129]](#cite_note-129)[[130]](#cite_note-130) and [J. K. Rowling](/wiki/J._K._Rowling "J. K. Rowling")[[131]](#cite_note-131)[[132]](#cite_note-132)[[133]](#cite_note-133) have often been featured in gender-critical social media posts. The Internet forum [Mumsnet](/wiki/Mumsnet "Mumsnet") has also been a prominent hub of online gender-critical discourse.[[134]](#cite_note-134)[[135]](#cite_note-135)

Gender-critical views are common in the British media.[[8]](#cite*note-Burns_2019-8)[[136]](#cite_note-cnn_uk_media-136) The British press frequently publishes articles critical of trans people and trans issues.[[136]](#cite_note-cnn_uk_media-136) In 2018, the US version of \_The Guardian* published an editorial condemning an editorial in the UK version of _The Guardian_ for transphobia, because it portrayed trans rights as being opposed to the rights of cis women.[[137]](#cite_note-us_guardian_response-137) Drawing on theory of [radicalization](/wiki/Radicalization "Radicalization"), Craig McLean argues that discourse on transgender-related issues in the UK has been radicalized in response to the activities of what he terms the anti-transgender movement that pushes "a radical agenda to deny the basic rights of trans people (...) under the cover of 'free speech'".[[138]](#cite_note-138)

In Resolution 2417 (2022), the [Council of Europe](/wiki/Council_of_Europe "Council of Europe") condemned "the highly prejudicial anti-gender, gender-critical and anti-trans narratives which reduce the fight for the equality of LGBTI people to what these movements deliberately mischaracterise as 'gender ideology' or 'LGBTI ideology'. Such narratives deny the very existence of LGBTI people, dehumanise them, and often falsely portray their rights as being in conflict with women's and children's rights, or societal and family values in general. All of these are deeply damaging to LGBTI people, while also harming women's and children's rights and social cohesion". The resolution further deplored "the extensive and often virulent attacks on the rights of LGBTI people that have been occurring for several years in, among other countries, Hungary, Poland, the Russian Federation, Turkey and the United Kingdom".[[24]](#cite_note-Resolution2417-24)[[139]](#cite_note-139)[[140]](#cite_note-140)

#### Legal cases

In 2019, the _[Maya Forstater v Center for Global Development](/w/index.php?title=Maya_Forstater_v_Center_for_Global_Development&action=edit&redlink=1 "Maya Forstater v Center for Global Development (page does not exist)")_ tribunal case was launched by [Maya Forstater](/wiki/Maya_Forstater "Maya Forstater"), crowdfunding over £120,000. Earlier that year, Forstater's consulting contract for the [Center for Global Development](/wiki/Center_for_Global_Development "Center for Global Development") was not renewed after she made a number of social media posts saying that men cannot change into women.[[148]](#cite_note-148) Forstater subsequently sued the Center, alleging that she had been discriminated against because of her views.[[149]](#cite_note-149) Forstater lost her initial case, with the judge ruling that her beliefs were not protected under the [Equality Act](/wiki/Equality_Act_2010 "Equality Act 2010") due to their absolutism. However, in April 2021, the initial judgement was reversed, with the [Employment Appeal Tribunal](/wiki/Employment_Appeal_Tribunal "Employment Appeal Tribunal") ruling that gender-critical beliefs were protected under the Equality Act.[[150]](#cite_note-150) A full merits hearing on Forstater's claim that she lost her employment as a result of these beliefs was heard in March 2022, and the decision, delivered in July 2022, was that Forstater had been subjected to direct discrimination and victimisation because of her gender-critical beliefs.[[151]](#cite_note-151)

In October 2020, Ann Sinnott, at the time a director of the [LGB Alliance](/wiki/LGB_Alliance "LGB Alliance"), initiated a legal case calling for a judicial review of the [Equality and Human Rights Commission](/wiki/Equality_and_Human_Rights_Commission "Equality and Human Rights Commission")'s guidance on the [Equality Act 2010](/wiki/Equality_Act_2010 "Equality Act 2010"), crowdfunding almost £100,000 for legal fees. In May 2021 the case was found by the court to be unarguable, Justice Henshaw stating that "the claimant has shown no arguable reason to believe the Code has misled or will mislead service providers about their responsibilities under the Act".[[152]](#cite_note-152)

The _Forstater_ case has been used as a precedent for several claims of discrimination against people holding gender-critical views. Employment tribunals have delivered successful judgements in cases against [a barrister's chambers](/wiki/Allison_Bailey_v_Stonewall_Equality_Ltd_%26_Others "Allison Bailey v Stonewall Equality Ltd & Others"), [Arts Council England](/wiki/Arts_Council_England "Arts Council England"), [Westminster Council](/wiki/Westminster_City_Council "Westminster City Council") and [Social Work England](/wiki/Social_Work_England "Social Work England"). Claims against [Girlguiding UK](/wiki/Girlguiding_UK "Girlguiding UK") and [United Kingdom Council for Psychotherapy](/wiki/United_Kingdom_Council_for_Psychotherapy "United Kingdom Council for Psychotherapy") ended in settlements, while a claim against the [Department for Work and Pensions](/wiki/Department_for_Work_and_Pensions "Department for Work and Pensions") failed after the claimant was deemed to have gone too far by misgendering service users. The barrister Georgiana Calvert-Lee commented to the _Guardian_: "Above all, in a pluralistic society, which is what we want, you have to accept that people are going to have different views."[[153]](#cite_note-153)

In January 2024, [Jo Phoenix](/wiki/Jo_Phoenix "Jo Phoenix") was successful in a claim against the [Open University](/wiki/Open_University "Open University") for discrimination on the grounds of gender-critical beliefs. The tribunal ruled that she had been constructively unfairly dismissed, and that she had suffered victimisation and harassment in the form of an open letter from 386 of her colleagues, as well as individual disparagement for her views, including one professor comparing her to "the racist uncle at the Christmas table".[[154]](#cite_note-154)

An anonymous claimant brought a legal action against a government lawyer, Elspeth Duemmer-Wrigley, accusing her of harassment, and against the [Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs](/wiki/Department_for_Environment,_Food_and_Rural_Affairs "Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs"), accusing it of fostering an "intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating and/or offensive environment" and called for the SEEN network to be dismantled. Duemmer-Wrigley is the chairwoman of the Sex Equality and Equity Network (SEEN). SEEN is a gender-critical group of more 700 civil servants from 50 government departments. In October 2023, leaders of SEEN, including Duemmer-Wrigley, wrote to the [Cabinet Secretary](</wiki/Cabinet_Secretary_(United_Kingdom)> "Cabinet Secretary (United Kingdom)") that gender critical views were being silenced within the civil service, putting impartiality at risk. In March 2024, the harassment case against Duemmer-Wrigley was dropped just before a tribunal.[[155]](#cite_note-155)

In August 2024, [Cambridgeshire County Council](/wiki/Cambridgeshire_County_Council "Cambridgeshire County Council") conceded that it had discriminated against Lizzie Pitt, a social worker, by initiating a disciplinary process against her following her gender-critical statements made at an LGBT support group. Pitt described the concession as a "win for the right side of history". The council admitted liability and agreed to pay compensation of £54,000.[[156]](#cite_note-156)[[157]](#cite_note-157)

In August 2024, a private settlement was reached between the Metanoia Institute and student psychotherapist James Esses. In a statement released following the settlement, the Metanoia Institute stated that it failed to follow its processes in not affording Esses a hearing prior to his expulsion after he expressed his gender-critical views and campaigned against a proposed ban on [conversion therapy](/wiki/Conversion_therapy "Conversion therapy"). In the statement, the institute apologized for publicizing the expulsion on social media.[[158]](#cite_note-158)[[159]](#cite_note-159)

In March 2025, _The Telegraph_ reported that [Northumbria Police](/wiki/Northumbria_Police "Northumbria Police") apologised to a gender-critical campaigner for "unacceptable" treatment during their investigation of an alleged hate crime.[[160]](#cite_note-160)

In April 2025, the [Supreme Court](/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom "Supreme Court of the United Kingdom") ruled in the case _[For Women Scotland Ltd v The Scottish Ministers](/wiki/For_Women_Scotland_Ltd_v_The_Scottish_Ministers "For Women Scotland Ltd v The Scottish Ministers")_ that "the terms woman and sex in the Equality Act 2010 refer to a biological woman and biological sex".[[161]](#cite_note-:15-161)

### United States

Although gender-critical feminism originated in the United States in the 1970s, it has largely fallen out of favor among American feminists.[[8]](#cite_note-Burns_2019-8) Some gender-critical organizations do remain, however, such as [WoLF](/wiki/WoLF "WoLF"), a gender-critical feminist organization that operates mainly within the United States.[[8]](#cite_note-Burns_2019-8)

## Analysis

### Scholarly analysis

[Lesbian studies](/wiki/Lesbian_studies "Lesbian studies") scholars Carly Thomsen and Laurie Essig note that "transness has been and is the object of deep hostility within some marginalized forms of feminism. Skepticism among earlier anti-trans feminists, such as Janice Raymond, about trans women being "real" women has morphed into J.K. Rowling's Twitter feed where she has insisted that trans women are not women. These ideas are, of course, deplorable, but they are also quite fringe within feminist studies and activism in the US".[[9]](#cite_note-Thomsen-9)

Clair Thurlow notes that the more explicitly hateful language used by early trans-exclusionary radical feminists failed to gain support, forcing them to pivot towards euphemisms and [dog-whistles](/wiki/Dog-whistle_politics "Dog-whistle politics") such as using "pro-woman" to mean "anti-trans", "protecting sex-based rights" meant excluding trans people, and "trans-exclusionary radical feminism" became "gender-critical feminism". This allowed trans-exclusionary feminism to appear reasonable to the average person while maintaining their anti-trans meanings to other anti-trans activists.[[1]](#cite_note-Thurlow-1)

Gender studies scholars Serena Bassi and Greta LaFleur have noted that TERFism started out as a fringe group among English speaking [cultural feminists](/wiki/Cultural_feminism "Cultural feminism") in the 1970s that grew rapidly due to media exposure.[[4]](#cite_note-Bassi-4)

Cristan Williams notes that radical feminism has historically been predominantly trans-inclusive and considers trans-exclusionary views a minority or fringe view within radical feminism.[[2]](#cite_note-Williams-2)

Carrera-Fernández and DePalma argued that "the increasingly belligerent popular discourses promoted by TERF groups since the 1970s [are] appropriating feminist discourses to produce arguments that contradict basic premises of feminism".[[162]](#cite_note-162)

[Henry F. Fradella](/wiki/Henry_F._Fradella "Henry F. Fradella") said that most contemporary feminists are supportive of trans people, and that gender-critical feminists are a small but vocal group who believe that trans rights threaten the rights of cis women. Most gender-critical arguments for this belief, he says, are false, and "misconstrue or ignore empirical data from both the natural and social sciences". Gender-critical feminism risks legal equality and contributes to criminalization of trans people.[[163]](#cite_note-163)

In July 2018, [Sally Hines](/wiki/Sally_Hines "Sally Hines"), a [University of Leeds](/wiki/University_of_Leeds "University of Leeds") professor of sociology and gender studies scholar, wrote in _[The Economist](/wiki/The_Economist "The Economist")_ that feminism and trans rights have been falsely portrayed as being in conflict by a minority of anti-transgender feminists, who often "reinforce the extremely offensive trope of the trans woman as a man in [drag](</wiki/Drag_(clothing)> "Drag (clothing)") who is a danger to women". Hines criticized these feminists for fueling "rhetoric of paranoia and hyperbole" against trans people, saying that they abandon or undermine feminist principles in their anti-trans narratives, such as [bodily autonomy](/wiki/Bodily_autonomy "Bodily autonomy") and self-determination of gender, and employ "reductive models of biology and restrictive understandings of the distinction between sex and gender" in defense of such narratives. She concluded with a call for explicit recognition of anti-transgender feminism as a violation of equality and dignity, and "a doctrine that runs counter to the ability to fulfill a liveable life or, often, a life at all".[[164]](#cite_note-Hines_2018-164)

Briar Dickey notes that "British 'trans-exclusionary radical feminist' (TERF) discourse has often been contextualised in fringe radical feminist thought", and argues that "the contextualisation of contemporary TERF discourse as an extension and evolution of fringe second-wave feminism [...] neglects its relationship to a wider international wave of anti-transgender sentiment" anchored in conservative and religious movements.[[10]](#cite_note-BD-10)

Researcher Aleardo Zanghellini argues that "gender-critical feminism advocates reserving women's spaces for [cis](/wiki/Cisgender "Cisgender") women" as well as that "Many problems in gender-critical thought are consistent with the explanation that paranoid structuralism is too often presupposed in gender-critical work".[[34]](#cite_note-Zanghellini_14-34)

[Mauro Cabral Grinspan](/wiki/Mauro_Cabral_Grinspan "Mauro Cabral Grinspan"), Ilana Eloit, [David Paternotte](/wiki/David_Paternotte "David Paternotte") and [Mieke Verloo](/wiki/Mieke_Verloo "Mieke Verloo") criticize the expression "gender-critical feminism" and argue that the term is problematic because it serves to rebrand anti-trans activism.[[35]](#cite_note-Grinspan-35)

Abbie E. Goldberg argues that "trans-exclusionary radical feminism (TERF) has contained similar [cisnormative](/wiki/Cisnormative "Cisnormative") arguments to those of social conservatives, promoting vilification of people with a trans lived experience in the guise of so-called gender-critical feminism" and that "this TERF approach has been used to promote exclusionary and discriminatory legislation, such as prohibiting equal access to public toilets and the right to be treated in accordance with one's gender in workplaces, accommodations, and public venues".[[165]](#cite*note-Goldberg-165)[*[page needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources")\_]

In a systematic review of TERF behaviour online, Nina Ploch found that TERFs exhibit similar group dynamics to conservative and authoritarian groups. These include a strong emphasis on group cohesion, rigid gender binaries, and the portrayal of trans individuals as an external threat, aligning with psychological traits such as [ethnocentrism](/wiki/Ethnocentrism "Ethnocentrism") and social dominance. Ploch noted that TERFs "show little openness to change, seek structure and order, and justify inequalities to cope with uncertainty and threat," further demonstrating "features of authoritarianism." According to her analysis, TERFs also tend to "operate within narrow circles of like-minded individuals" and are often "convinced of their moral superiority."[[166]](#cite_note-166)

### Relationship with the anti-gender movement

Bassi and LaFleur write that "the trans-exclusionary feminist (TERF) movement and the so-called anti-gender movement are only rarely distinguished as movements with distinct constitutions and aims".[[4]](#cite_note-Bassi-4) Pearce et al. note that the concept of "[gender ideology](/wiki/Gender_ideology "Gender ideology")" "saw increasing circulation in [trans-exclusionary radical feminist](/wiki/Trans-exclusionary_radical_feminist "Trans-exclusionary radical feminist") discourse" from around 2016.[[5]](#cite_note-Pearce-5) Claire House noted in 2023 that "key streams within trans exclusionary women's and feminist movements increasingly engage in collaborative action with right-wing populist-centered anti-gender coalitions, which include right-wing religious, conservative, and right-wing extremist actors".[[167]](#cite_note-167) Claire Thurlow writes that "despite efforts to obscure the point, gender critical feminism continues to rely on transphobic tropes, moral panics and essentialist understandings of men and women. These factors also continue to link trans-exclusionary feminism to anti-feminist reactionary politics and other 'anti-gender' movements".[[1]](#cite_note-Thurlow-1)

[UN Women](/wiki/UN_Women "UN Women") has described the gender-critical, anti-gender and [men's rights movements](/wiki/Men%27s_rights_movement "Men's rights movement") as [anti-rights movements](/wiki/Anti-rights_movements "Anti-rights movements") that overlap in opposition to what they describe as "gender ideology", which the agency described as "a term used to oppose the concept of gender, women's rights, and the rights of LGBTIQ+ people broadly." They argued these groups have attempted to "frame equality for women and LGBTIQ+ people as a threat to so-called 'traditional' family values" and linked them to "hateful propaganda and disinformation to target and attempt to delegitimize people with diverse sexual orientations, gender identities, gender expressions, and sex characteristics."[[25]](#cite_note-unwomen-25)[[26]](#cite_note-thetimesantirights-26)

### Political alliances with conservatives and the far right

Some trans-exclusionary radical feminists have allied with conservative or far-right groups and politicians who oppose legislation that would expand transgender rights in the United States.[[168]](#cite_note-nbcnews_2019-01-29-168)[[169]](#cite_note-buzzfeed_2019-04-02-169) According to [der Freitag](/wiki/Der_Freitag "Der Freitag"): "TERF positions are now mostly heard from conservatives and right-wing extremists."[[21]](#cite_note-Studnik-21)

Feminist philosopher [Judith Butler](/wiki/Judith_Butler "Judith Butler") has described the anti-gender movements as [fascist](/wiki/Fascist "Fascist") trends and cautioned self-declared feminists from allying with such movements in targeting trans, [non-binary](/wiki/Non-binary "Non-binary"), and genderqueer people.[[18]](#cite*note-Butler_idea_of*'gender'_backlash-18) Butler said that "it is painful to see that Trump's position that gender should be defined by biological sex, and that the evangelical and right-wing Catholic effort to purge 'gender' from education and public policy accords with the trans-exclusionary radical feminists' return to biological essentialism".[[170]](#cite_note-170) Sophia Siddiqui, the deputy editor of \*[Race & Class](/wiki/Race_%26_Class "Race & Class")\*, has argued that "'gender critical' feminists play into the hands of far-right street forces and extreme-right electoral parties which would like to abolish anti-discrimination protections altogether" and that it "could have a damaging effect on global feminist and LGBT movements by reinforcing conservative ideas about gender and sexuality".[[171]](#cite_note-171) The [Canadian Anti-Hate Network](/wiki/Canadian_Anti-Hate_Network "Canadian Anti-Hate Network") said that despite labelling themselves as feminists, TERF groups often collaborate with conservative and far-right groups.[[19]](#cite_note-CTV_hate_rise_Canada-19) Serena Bassi and Greta LaFleur note that "gender-critical movements often reemploy the well-known right-wing populist opposition between 'the corrupt global elites' and 'the people'", noting the similarity of gender-critical beliefs to "far-right conspiracy theorizing".[[4]](#cite_note-Bassi-4) In a direct response to Butler, Sarah Lambert cautioned against conflating gender-critical feminism and right-wing politics or neo-fascism, arguing that some gender critical feminist groups in Britain "align openly with the far-right", that "other prominent advocates hold left-wing or liberal positions", and that some groups are non-partisan.[[172]](#cite_note-172)

Gender studies scholar C. Libby has pointed to "burgeoning connections between trans-exclusionary radical feminism, "gender critical" writing, and transphobic evangelical Christian rhetoric".[[173]](#cite_note-173)

In January 2019, [The Heritage Foundation](/wiki/The_Heritage_Foundation "The Heritage Foundation"), an American conservative think tank, hosted a panel of self-described radical feminists opposed to the US [Equality Act](</wiki/Equality_Act_(United_States)> "Equality Act (United States)").[[168]](#cite_note-nbcnews_2019-01-29-168) Heron Greenesmith of [Political Research Associates](/wiki/Political_Research_Associates "Political Research Associates"), an American liberal think tank, has said that the latest iteration of collaboration between conservatives and anti-transgender feminists is in part a reaction to the trans community's "incredible gains" in civil rights and visibility, and that anti-trans feminists and conservatives capitalize on a "scarcity mindset rhetoric" whereby civil rights are portrayed as a limited commodity and must be prioritized to cisgender women over other groups. Greenesmith compared this rhetoric to the right-wing tactic of prioritizing the rights of citizens over non-citizens and white people over people of colour.[[168]](#cite_note-nbcnews_2019-01-29-168) Bev Jackson, one of the founders of the [LGB Alliance](/wiki/LGB_Alliance "LGB Alliance"), has argued in contrast that "working with The Heritage Foundation is sometimes the only possible course of action" since "the leftwing silence on gender in the US is even worse than in the UK".[[174]](#cite_note-174)

In a 2020 article in _[Lambda Nordica](/wiki/Lambda_Nordica "Lambda Nordica")_, [Erika Alm](/wiki/Erika_Alm "Erika Alm") of the [University of Gothenburg](/wiki/University_of_Gothenburg "University of Gothenburg") and [Elisabeth L. Engebretsen](/wiki/Elisabeth_L._Engebretsen "Elisabeth L. Engebretsen") of the [University of Stavanger](/wiki/University_of_Stavanger "University of Stavanger"), said that there was "growing convergence, and sometimes conscious alliances, between "gender-critical" feminists (sometimes known as TERFs – Trans-Exclusionary Radical Feminists), religious and social conservatives, as well as right-wing politics and even neo-Nazi and fascist movements" and that the convergence was linked to "their reliance on an essentialised and binary understanding of sex and/or gender, often termed 'bio-essentialism'".[[20]](#cite*note-Alm*&\_Engebretsen*2020-20) Engebretsen has described the movement as a "complex threat to democracy".[[175]](#cite_note-Engebretsen-175) Another 2020 article, in *[The Sociological Review](/wiki/The_Sociological_Review "The Sociological Review")\_, said that "the language of 'gender ideology' originates in anti-feminist and anti-trans discourses among right-wing Christians, with the Catholic Church acting as a major nucleating agent", and said that the term "saw increasing circulation in trans-exclusionary radical feminist discourse" from around 2016. It further said that "a growing number of anti-trans campaigners associated with radical feminist movements have openly aligned themselves with anti-feminist organisations".[[5]](#cite_note-Pearce-5)

In a 2021 paper in _[Signs: Journal of Women in Culture and Society](/wiki/Signs:_Journal_of_Women_in_Culture_and_Society "Signs: Journal of Women in Culture and Society")_, Hil Malatino of [Pennsylvania State University](/wiki/Pennsylvania_State_University "Pennsylvania State University") said that "'gender-critical' feminism" in the US has "begun to build coalition with the evangelical Right around the legal codification of sex as a biological binary" and that "popular news media frames transphobia as part of a rational, enlightened, pragmatic response to what is variously called the 'trans lobby' and the 'cult of trans'".[[176]](#cite*note-176) Another 2021 paper, in *[Law and Social Inquiry](/wiki/Law_and_Social_Inquiry "Law and Social Inquiry")\_, said that "a coalition of Christian conservative legal organizations, conservative foundations, Trump administration officials, Republican party lawmakers, and trans-exclusionary radical feminists has assembled to redefine the right to privacy in service of anti-transgender politics" and that "social conservatives have cast the issue as one of balancing two competing rights claims rather than one of outright animus against a gender minority population".[[177]](#cite_note-177)

### Misinformation and disinformation

Political communication scholar TJ Billard has argued that "[misinformation](/wiki/Misinformation "Misinformation")—or, more specifically, [disinformation](/wiki/Disinformation "Disinformation")—about trans topics has become the defining feature of public discourse on transgender rights."[[14]](#cite_note-:1-14) Cilia Williams et al. noted in an article on gender critical feminist discourse in Spain that "anti-trans narratives online [...] use attacks, misinformation, and self-defence as a communication strategy, rather than debate or dialogue."[[15]](#cite_note-:2-15) Gender theorist Alyosxa Tudor has written that "strategic disinformation as [an] accelerator" has been used to push forward "hateful and anti-democratic agendas."[[16]](#cite_note-:3-16)

Conflict between gender-critical feminists and other feminists and transgender rights activists has resulted in controversies in which the principles of academic freedom have been invoked. Conflicts have erupted at university campuses.

In July 2025, Alice Sullivan, a gender critical feminist and Professor of Sociology, published a report commissioned by the previous Conservative government accusing UK universities of not protecting gender critical academics from bullying and restrictions on research [[178]](#cite*note-178). Multiple cases were cited in the report and have been described in the media in which academics engaged in gender critical research have claimed unfair treatment [[179]](#cite_note-179). For example, in September 2022, [Laura Favaro](/w/index.php?title=Laura_Favaro&action=edit&redlink=1 "Laura Favaro (page does not exist)"), a research fellow at [City, University of London](/wiki/City,_University_of_London "City, University of London")'s Gender and Sexualities Research Centre, published an article in *[Times Higher Education](/wiki/Times_Higher_Education "Times Higher Education")\_ discussing her research into the climate of the debate among academics. Noting that she had interviewed 50 feminist academics in gender studies with a range of views on the subject, Favaro stated "my discussions left me in no doubt that a culture of discrimination, silencing and fear has taken hold across universities in England, and many countries beyond".[[180]](#cite_note-180) Favaro later began discrimination proceedings against City University, stating she had been "ostracised at her workplace and denied access to her research data" after the publication of her article.[[181]](#cite_note-181)[[182]](#cite_note-:8-182) City University responded with a statement that it had a "legal obligation to protect freedom of expression that we take very seriously". It also took its "obligations with respect to ethics and integrity very seriously" and made clear that "any personal data processed in the course of any research [should be] processed in compliance with data protection legislation".[[182]](#cite_note-:8-182)

### Conflicts with other feminist and pro-equality groups

In February 2020, 28 feminist and LGBT groups in France co-signed a declaration titled _Toutes des femmes_ denouncing trans-exclusionary feminism, saying that "questions disguised as 'legitimate concerns' quickly give way to more violent attacks" and that "it is a confusionist and conspiratorial ideological movement using the cover of feminism to disrupt real feminist fights.[[183]](#cite_note-183) The declaration has since also been signed by over 100 additional feminist, LGBT, and progressive groups.[[23]](#cite_note-Toutes_des_femmes-23) In May 2021, over 110 women's and human rights organisations in Canada signed a statement stating that they "vehemently reject the dangerous and bigoted rhetoric and ideology espoused by Trans Exclusionary Radical 'Feminists' (TERFs)", and saying that "trans people are a driving force in our feminist movements and make incredible contributions across all facets of our society".[[22]](#cite_note-Canada_110-22)

[Judith Butler](/wiki/Judith_Butler "Judith Butler") said in 2020 that trans-exclusionary radical feminism is "a fringe movement that is seeking to speak in the name of the mainstream, and that our responsibility is to refuse to let that happen".[[184]](#cite_note-184)

In 2021, the [Council of Europe](/wiki/Council_of_Europe "Council of Europe") Committee on Equality and Non-Discrimination published a report titled _Combating rising hate against LGBTI people in Europe_, which condemned "the highly prejudicial anti-gender, gender-critical and anti-trans narratives which reduce the fight for the equality of LGBTI people to what these movements deliberately mischaracterise as '[gender ideology](/wiki/Gender_ideology "Gender ideology")' or '[LGBTI ideology](/wiki/LGBT_%22ideology%22 'LGBT "ideology"')'" and which said there was "a direct link between heteronormativity and heterosexism, on the one hand, and the growing anti-gender and gender-critical movements".[[17]](#cite_note-Council_of_Europe_2021-17) The report formed the basis of Resolution 2417, adopted in January 2022.[[24]](#cite_note-Resolution2417-24)

In late-January 2018, over 1000 Irish feminists, including several groups such as the [University College Dublin](/wiki/University_College_Dublin "University College Dublin") Centre of Gender, Feminisms & Sexualities, signed an open letter condemning a planned meeting in Ireland on UK Gender Recognition Act reforms organised by a British group opposing the reforms.[[185]](#cite_note-185) The letter stated that "[t]rans people and particularly trans women are an inextricable part of our feminist community" and accused the British group of colonialism.[[186]](#cite_note-186)

Sociologist Kelsy Burke argued that "TERFs aren't aligned with most feminists" and wrote that "most American feminists are far from trans-exclusionary and have long been among the most supportive groups of LGBTQ equality".[[187]](#cite_note-187)

### Social media

The controversial Reddit community [r/GenderCritical](/wiki/R/GenderCritical "R/GenderCritical") gathered a reputation as an anti-trans space. In June 2020, it was banned abruptly for violating new rules against "promoting hate". Members set up a similar community called Ovarit.[[188]](#cite_note-:5-188)

## Symbolism and iconography

The colors purple, green, and white, originally associated with the [British women's suffrage movement](/wiki/Women%27s_suffrage_in_the_United_Kingdom "Women's suffrage in the United Kingdom"), have been adopted by many gender-critical feminist groups. Sarah Pedersen writes that "use of suffragette pen names and WSPU colours allows gender-critical posters to identify each other both on and offline."[[189]](#cite_note-189)
These colors are frequently used in logos, promotional material, and online bios, often evoking a sense of historical feminist legitimacy. However, this color scheme has increasingly been criticized as a visual marker for trans-exclusionary ideologies.[[190]](#cite_note-190)
